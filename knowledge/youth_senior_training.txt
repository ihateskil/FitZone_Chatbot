===== YOUTH & SENIOR TRAINING GUIDE =====

SECTION 1: YOUTH FITNESS BASICS (AGES 6-12)
- Physical activity recommendations: Children 6-12 need at least 60 minutes of moderate-to-vigorous physical activity daily. This can be accumulated throughout the day (doesn't need to be consecutive). Includes aerobic, muscle-strengthening, and bone-strengthening activities at least 3 days per week.
- Fundamental movement skills (FMS): The building blocks of all physical activity. Categories: Locomotor (running, hopping, skipping, jumping, galloping, sliding), Object control (throwing, catching, kicking, striking, bouncing), Stability (balancing, twisting, turning, landing, dodging). Children who master FMS are 3x more likely to be active adults.
- Physical literacy: The motivation, confidence, physical competence, knowledge, and understanding to value and take responsibility for engagement in physical activities for life. Developed through diverse movement experiences in supportive environments.
- The Long-Term Athletic Development (LTAD) model: A framework for developing physical activity and sport participation across the lifespan. Developed by Canadian Sport for Life. Key stages: Active Start (0-6), FUNdamentals (6-9 girls, 6-9 boys), Learn to Train (8-11 girls, 9-12 boys), Train to Train (11-15 girls, 12-16 boys), Train to Compete (15-21 girls, 16-23 boys), Train to Win (18+), Active for Life (any age).
- Windows of trainability: Critical periods in child development where specific fitness components are most responsive to training. Flexibility (6-10), Speed (7-12 girls, 7-12 boys), Skill (1-10). Aerobic endurance (onset of PHV), Strength (12-24 months after PHV).
- Youth sports participation: Focus on fun, skill development, and social connection over winning. Multiple sport participation is associated with better long-term athletic development and lower injury rates. Early specialization (before age 12) is linked to overuse injuries, burnout, and dropout.
- Play-based training: Unstructured active play is the foundation of physical development. Organized sport should complement, not replace, free play. Tag, obstacle courses, capture the flag, climbing trees, and imaginative movement games all build physical competence.
- Common youth fitness program elements: Warm-up games (5-10 min), skill development (15-20 min), strength/power (10-15 min bodyweight), aerobic play (10-15 min), cool-down games with stretching (5 min). Emphasis on maximal participation (no elimination games).
- Risks of youth training: Overuse injuries (Osgood-Schlatter, Sever's disease, stress fractures) from excessive sport-specific training without rest. Growth plate injuries from improper lifting technique. Heat illness from inadequate hydration. Burnout from excessive competition and pressure.
- Sample youth workout (ages 8-12): Warm-up: "animal walks" (bear crawl, crab walk, frog jumps). Main: obstacle course with crawling, balancing, jumping, throwing. Strength: bodyweight circuits (squats, push-ups on knees, bird dogs, glute bridges, planks). Game: tag variations. Cool-down: partner stretching.

SECTION 2: YOUTH STRENGTH TRAINING
- Is strength training safe for children and adolescents? Yes, when properly supervised and designed. The American Academy of Pediatrics, ACSM, and NSCA all support youth strength training with appropriate guidelines. Injury rates are lower than many team sports when proper form is emphasized.
- Children CAN increase strength: Before puberty, strength gains are primarily neurological (improved motor unit recruitment, coordination). After puberty, hormonal changes allow muscle hypertrophy. A 12-week program can produce 30-50% strength increases in pre-pubescent children through neural adaptations.
- Age-appropriate resistance training guidelines:
  * Ages 6-8: Bodyweight exercises, stability ball, medicine ball (1-2 lbs), resistance bands. Focus on balance, coordination, and fundamental movements.
  * Ages 9-11: Add light dumbbells (2-5 lbs), kettlebells (8-12 lbs), and machines with strict supervision. Introduce compound movements with light loads.
  * Ages 12-15: Progress to more structured program. Barbell techniques (bodyweight bar or light load). Continue to emphasize form over load.
  * Ages 16+: Can follow adult programs with appropriate loads. Still prioritize technique.
- Key exercises for youth: Squat (bodyweight → goblet → barbell), Push-ups (wall → incline → full), Lunges (forward, lateral, reverse), Pull-ups (negatives, band-assisted, or rows), Planks (front, side, bear crawl holds), Bridges (glute, single leg), Bird dogs, Dead bugs.
- Program design for youth: 2-3 sessions per week on non-consecutive days. 6-10 exercises per session. 1-3 sets per exercise. 8-15 reps per set (fewer reps for power work). Progress load by 5-10% when 15 reps can be completed with good form.
- PHV (Peak Height Velocity): The period of fastest growth during puberty. Typically age 12 for girls, 14 for boys. During PHV, coordination temporarily decreases (growth spurt). Avoid heavy loading of the spine during rapid trunk growth. Emphasize flexibility and motor control. After PHV, strength training becomes more effective due to hormonal changes.
- Unique considerations for female adolescents: Increased risk of ACL injury starting at PHV due to neuromuscular control differences. ACL prevention programs (jump training, landing mechanics, plyometrics) are effective. Triple extension (hip, knee, ankle) mechanics during jumping should be trained.
- Sport sampling vs specialization: Encourage participation in multiple sports before age 12. Specialization before puberty increases risk of overuse injury, psychological burnout, and missed skill development. Many professional athletes played multiple sports as youth.
- When to introduce weights: Bodyweight mastery before external loading. Ability to perform 10 perfect bodyweight squats, 5 perfect push-ups, 30-second perfect plank. Understanding of exercise concepts (reps, sets, tempo, rest). Enough attention span for instruction (usually age 7-8 minimum).
- Youth warm-up: 5-10 minutes dynamic movement (jumping jacks, high knees, butt kicks, leg swings, arm circles, torso twists, cat-cows, inchworms). Fun is more important than specificity. Games like Simon Says or follow-the-leader work well.
- Youth programming example (ages 10-12, 3x/week):
  Session A: Goblet squat 3x10, Push-ups (or incline) 3x5-10, Single leg RDL to touch 3x8 each, Bird dog 3x8 each, Bear crawl 3x15s, Farmer carry 3x30s
  Session B: Bodyweight walking lunge 3x8 each, DB overhead press 2x8, Glute bridge 3x12, Lateral lunge 3x6 each, Dead bug 3x8 each, Plank 3x20s
  Session C: Box jumps (low box) 3x5, DB rows 3x10 each, Reverse lunges 3x8 each, Side plank 3x15s each, Crawl pattern drill 3x10s, Supine band pull-apart 3x12

SECTION 3: OLDER ADULT BASICS (AGES 65+)
- Physical activity recommendations for older adults (65+): At least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity OR 75 minutes of vigorous-intensity aerobic activity per week (or combination). Muscle-strengthening activities at least 2 days per week. Balance training at least 3 days per week for fall prevention. Older adults with chronic conditions should be as active as their condition allows.
- The WHO older adult activity guidelines: Additional emphasis on functional balance and strength training (3+ days per week) to enhance functional capacity and prevent falls. Multicomponent physical activity that includes balance, strength, aerobic, and flexibility training is recommended.
- Aging physiology: Muscle mass declines 3-8% per decade after age 30, accelerating after 60 (sarcopenia). Bone density declines 0.5-1% per year after age 40 (accelerates in women after menopause). VO2 max declines 10% per decade (slowed by training). Neural processing speed declines. Balance deteriorates.
- Sarcopenia: Age-related loss of muscle mass and function. Affects 10-16% of older adults worldwide. Accelerates after age 70. Consequences: mobility impairment, falls, loss of independence, metabolic dysfunction, mortality. Reversible (or at least improvable) with resistance training.
- Successful aging frameworks: Rowe & Kahn model (low disease risk, high cognitive/ physical function, active engagement with life). WHO's concept of intrinsic capacity (composite of physical and mental capacities). Strength, balance, mobility, cognition, and sensory function all contribute.
- Frailty: A geriatric syndrome characterized by reduced physiological reserve and increased vulnerability to stressors. Fried frailty phenotype: weight loss, exhaustion, weakness (low grip strength), slow walking speed, low physical activity. Frailty is modifiable through exercise and nutrition.
- Functional fitness: The ability to perform activities of daily living (ADLs) safely and independently. Key components: lower body strength (getting out of chairs, climbing stairs), upper body strength (carrying groceries, lifting objects), aerobic endurance (walking distances, housework), balance (preventing falls), flexibility (reaching, bending).
- Benefits of exercise for older adults: Increased muscle mass and strength (even in 90+ year olds), improved bone density, better balance and reduced fall risk (30-50% reduction in falls), improved cardiovascular health, reduced risk of cognitive decline (30% reduction in dementia risk), better mood (reduces depression risk), improved sleep quality, enhanced immune function.
- Exercise timing for older adults: Avoid exercising vigorously within 1 hour of going to bed. Morning exercise may be best for circadian rhythm and sleep quality. Consider medication timing (e.g., insulin, blood pressure meds may affect exercise response). Allow adequate warm-up (10-15 minutes) for tissues to become pliable.
- Contraindications and precautions: Unstable cardiovascular disease (angina, recent MI, uncontrolled arrhythmias), severe aortic stenosis, acute illness or fever, severe orthopedic injury, uncontrolled hypertension (>180/110). Always obtain medical clearance before starting a new exercise program. Stop exercise for: chest pain, dizziness, severe shortness of breath, joint pain, extreme fatigue.

SECTION 4: STRENGTH TRAINING FOR OLDER ADULTS
- Is it too late to start? Research is clear: older adults respond well to resistance training at any age. Gains of 50-100% in strength are possible in 3-6 months. Never too late to start. The oldest age studied in training interventions: 98 years old (gained strength and function).
- Key principles: Start conservatively (low load, high reps), progress slowly (2-3 weeks at each weight), prioritize functional movements (sit-to-stand, step-ups, reaching), emphasize proper form and breathing (no Valsalva maneuver), include both lower and upper body, train through full range of motion (if pain-free).
- Essential exercises for older adults: Chair sit-to-stand (foundational leg exercise), Wall push-ups (upper body pushing), Seated row (or band pull) for upper back and posture, Hip hinge to seated (glute/hamstring activation), Step-ups (to a low platform with support), Heel raises (ankle strength and balance), Farmer carries (grip strength, core, posture).
- Load selection: Start at 40-60% of 1RM (approximately 12-15 rep max). Progress to 60-75% (8-12 rep range) for strength gains. Use RPE scale 6-8 (leave 2-4 reps in reserve). Lower body progressions: bodyweight → light dumbbells → barbell/heavier loads.
- Frequency: 2-3 sessions per week on non-consecutive days. Even 1 session per week maintains strength. 2-3 sessions produces optimal gains. Sessions should be 30-45 minutes including warm-up and cool-down.
- Hypertrophy for older adults: Possible but slower than in younger adults. Adequate protein intake (1.2-1.6 g/kg/day) is crucial. Sufficient volume (2-4 sets per exercise). Some post-exercise muscle soreness is normal and indicates adaptation.
- Blood pressure considerations: Avoid the Valsalva maneuver (holding breath while straining). Exhale on exertion. Avoid very heavy loads (less than 6 reps per set). Use machines or supported positions when needed. Take longer rest periods (90 sec - 2 min). Monitor for dizziness or lightheadedness.
- Joint protection: Use neutral grip options (palms facing each other) for pulling exercises. Machine-based exercises can be safer for arthritic joints (controlled path of motion). Avoid deep squatting if hip or knee arthritis. Box squats allow controlled depth and rest point.
- Periodization for older adults: Linear periodization (increase load, decrease reps over 4-6 weeks) works well. Undulating periodization (vary heavy/light days within a week) is also effective, as is daily undulating periodization. Deload every 4-6 weeks (reduce volume 30-50%). More frequent deloads may be needed.
- Sample older adult program (Beginner, 2x/week):
  Warm-up (10 min): March in place 2 min, ankle circles, arm circles, trunk twists, cat-cow, hip circles
  Strength (25 min):
  1. Chair sit-to-stand 2x10-15
  2. Wall push-ups 2x8-12
  3. Seated band row 2x10-12
  4. Standing hip hinge 2x10 (supported)
  5. Step-ups 2x8 each (low step, hold rail)
  6. Heel raises 2x12 (hold counter)
  7. Farmer carry 2x30 sec (light dumbbells)
  Cool-down: Full body static stretches 5 min (hold each 20-30 sec)

SECTION 5: CARDIOVASCULAR TRAINING FOR OLDER ADULTS
- Exercise modality: Low impact preferred: walking (brisk pace), stationary cycling, swimming, water aerobics, elliptical trainer, recumbent stepper, rowing machine. Walking is the safest and most accessible. Minimum pace: 3 mph (20 min/mile) for moderate intensity.
- Walking program for older adults: Start with 10-15 minutes at conversational pace, 3-5 days/week. Increase by 2-5 minutes per week. Goal: 30 minutes of continuous walking at moderate intensity (slightly breathless but able to talk). Nordic walking (using poles) burns 20% more calories, reduces knee stress, and improves upper body conditioning.
- Water aerobics: Excellent for arthritis, joint pain, or balance concerns. Buoyancy reduces joint stress by 80-90%. Resistance from water builds strength. Water temperature: 83-88°F. Classes improve social connection (important for adherence).
- Stationary cycling: Lower joint impact than walking or running. Recumbent bikes offer more back support. Seated cycling is superior to standing climbs for older adults. Monitor HR and RPE. Interval training on bike: 2 min moderate, 1 min faster, repeat 5-10 times.
- Heart rate considerations: Age-predicted HRmax (e.g., Tanaka: 208 - 0.7×age) may be less accurate for older adults. RPE (6-20 Borg scale) is more reliable. Talk test is simple: moderate = can talk but not sing, vigorous = can't say more than a few words without pausing for breath.
- Balance considerations during cardio: Use handrails on treadmills (don't hold tightly — this changes gait). Avoid aggressive incline walking. Stationary bike offers no fall risk (best for balance-impaired). Water walking eliminates fall risk entirely.
- Daily step goal for older adults: 7,000-10,000 steps per day is associated with lower mortality. Even 4,400 steps per day shows benefit compared to 2,700. Step intensity matters: 30 minutes of purposeful walking (not incidental steps) provides the most health benefit.
- Cardiovascular adaptations in older adults: Heart becomes more compliant (fills better), stroke volume increases, resting HR decreases, blood pressure decreases (5-10 mmHg systolic), blood lipid profile improves (HDL up, LDL down, triglycerides down), glucose metabolism improves.
- HIIT for seniors: Modified HIIT effective for cardiovascular health. Protocol: 3:1 or 4:1 work-to-rest ratio. Work intervals: 30-60 seconds at "somewhat hard" (RPE 5-7/10). Rest: 1-2 minutes active recovery. Total work time: 10-20 minutes. Example: walk faster 45 sec, walk slow 90 sec, repeat 6-10 times.

SECTION 6: MOBILITY, BALANCE & FLEXIBILITY
- Static stretching: Hold a stretch at the end range of motion for 15-60 seconds. Best performed after exercise (when muscles are warm). Reduces muscle tension, increases ROM. Not recommended before explosive activities (can temporarily reduce power output by 2-5%).
- Dynamic stretching: Controlled movement through full range of motion. Best before exercise to prepare joints and increase blood flow. Examples: leg swings, arm circles, torso twists, hip circles, walking lunges with rotation. Improves performance and reduces injury risk.
- PNF stretching (proprioceptive neuromuscular facilitation): Involves alternating contraction and relaxation of target muscle. Hold-relax: stretch target, isometric contraction 5-10 seconds, relax, deepen stretch. Most effective for immediate ROM gains. Can increase ROM by 20-30% in a single session.
- Yoga for older adults: Improves flexibility, balance, strength, and mental well-being. Chair yoga (seated modifications) is accessible for those with limited standing ability. Gentle Hatha yoga (slow pace, long holds) is best. Avoid inverted poses if high blood pressure (BP) or glaucoma. Avoid deep twists if spine issues. Props (blocks, straps, blankets) are encouraged.
- Tai Chi: "Meditation in motion." Combines slow, deliberate movements with deep breathing and mental focus. Excellent for balance (reduces falls by 43-50% in older adults). Evidence-based: proven to improve balance, reduce falls, reduce pain in fibromyalgia/knee osteoarthritis. Sun style is gentler than Yang or Chen.
- Balance training fundamentals: Challenge the 3 sensory systems: visual (close eyes), vestibular (head turns), somatosensory (unstable surfaces). Progressions: wide base → narrow base → tandem → single leg. Stable surface → foam/cushion → wobble board. Eyes open → eyes closed. Still → moving.
- Functional balance exercises: Standing on one foot (start 10s, progress to 30s), Heel-toe walking (8-10 steps forward and back), Tandem stance (one foot directly in front of the other), Walking with head turns (look left then right as you walk), Sidestepping along a line, Standing and reaching in different directions (without moving feet), Clock reach (reach to 12, 3, 6, 9 o'clock positions).
- Fall prevention ladder: Level 1: Sit-to-stand (strength + balance). Level 2: Standing balance with support. Level 3: Standing balance without support. Level 4: Dynamic balance (walking with variations). Level 5: Perturbation-based balance (recovering from pushes/uneven surfaces).
- Arthritis and exercise: Low-impact activities (walking, swimming, cycling) reduce pain and stiffness. Strength training around the joint reduces pain. Flexibility exercises maintain range of motion. Avoid high-impact activities during flare-ups. Heat before exercise (warm bath, heating pad) to reduce stiffness; ice after if needed.
- Flexibility by region: Hip flexors (tight from sitting): low lunge, half pigeon. Hamstrings (tight from sitting): seated forward fold, supine hamstring stretch. Chest/shoulders (tight from rounded posture): doorway stretch, prone cobra. Lower back (tight/stiff): cat-cow, child's pose, knee-to-chest.

SECTION 7: CHRONIC CONDITIONS & EXERCISE
- Type 2 diabetes: Exercise increases insulin sensitivity (glucose uptake into muscles without insulin). Benefits last 24-72 hours post-exercise. Best combination: aerobic (30 min moderate, 5 days/week) + resistance training (2-3 days/week). Check blood glucose before exercise: <100 mg/dL → eat snack, >250 mg/dL with ketones → postpone. Stay hydrated. Watch for hypoglycemia during and after exercise.
- Osteoporosis: Weight-bearing exercise stimulates bone formation. Effective: Walking, jogging (if appropriate), stair climbing, resistance training, jumping (if safe). Avoid: Torsional loading of spine (don't do oblique twists with weight), heavy overhead lifting with spine extension, forward flexion (bending over to touch toes) — can cause compression fractures.
- Cardiovascular disease: Cardiac rehab programs include monitored exercise. Guidelines: Aerobic 3-5 days/week, 20-60 minutes, moderate intensity (RPE 11-13/20, talk test). Resistance training 2-3 days/week, moderate intensity (10-15 reps). Monitor HR and RPE. Warning signs to stop: chest pain, shortness of breath, dizziness, palpitations.
- Hypertension: Exercise lowers BP 5-10 mmHg. Moderate-intensity aerobic exercise (40-60% HRR) is most effective. Avoid heavy resistance training (very high intensity). Avoid Valsalva maneuver (exhale on exertion). Isometric handgrip training (2 min squeeze, 1 min rest × 4) shown to lower BP.
- COPD: Pulmonary rehab dramatically improves quality of life. Interval training (1-3 min work, 1-2 min rest) is better than continuous exercise. Pursed-lip breathing during exertion. Upper body strength training improves arm function for ADLs. Monitor O2 saturation (>90%), stop if significant desaturation.
- Cognitive impairment / Dementia: Regular exercise reduces risk of cognitive decline by 30%. Aerobic exercise increases BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor) which supports neural health. Walking programs reduce agitation in dementia patients. Exercise combined with cognitive tasks (e.g., walking while counting backwards) is more beneficial.
- Parkinson's disease: Boxing-style training improves coordination and cognitive function. Cycling (especially forced-rate cycling on stationary bike) reduces symptoms. Gait training: walking with cues (metronome, stripes on floor). Balance training reduces falls. Treadmill walking improves gait speed and stride length.
- Fibromyalgia: Low-impact aerobic exercise (walking, swimming, tai chi) reduces pain. Start at very low intensity (5-10 minutes), advance slowly. Pain may increase initially but decreases over 6-12 weeks. Avoid high-impact exercise. Warm water exercise (pool temp 86-90°F) is particularly beneficial.
- Arthritis (OA and RA): Low-impact exercise reduces pain more than medication alone. Hand exercises (squeeze putty, finger spreads) for hand OA. Quadriceps strengthening for knee OA. Hip strengthening for hip OA. Water exercise for full-body movement without joint stress. Rest between workouts (joints need recovery).

SECTION 8: PROGRAM DESIGN FOR OLDER ADULTS
- FITT-VP principle for older adults: Frequency (aerobic 3-5 days + resistance 2-3 days + flexibility/balance 2-7 days), Intensity (moderate = talk test, RPE 5-6/10 for aerobic; 50-80% 1RM for resistance 1 set to start, progress to 2-3 sets), Time (30 min of aerobic accumulating to 150+ min/week), Type (multi-component: aerobic, resistance, flexibility, balance), Volume (start low, progress 5% per week), Progression (2-3 weeks between progressions).
- Initial assessment: Medical clearance (especially for chronic conditions), Functional tests: 30-sec chair stand (lower body strength), 8-ft up-and-go (mobility/balance), 6-min walk (aerobic endurance), arm curl (upper body strength), back scratch (shoulder flexibility), chair sit-and-reach (hamstring flexibility).
- Motivation and adherence: Social connection (classes/partners) is the strongest predictor of adherence. Autonomy (choose exercises, set own goals). Perceived competence (programs matched to ability). Positive feedback (celebrate small wins). Habit stacking (link exercise to existing routine). Identify and overcome barriers (transport, weather, health flare-ups).
- Group exercise considerations: Instructor must monitor for signs of distress. Provide modifications (low, medium, high options for each exercise). Use clear, simple instructions (repeat and demonstrate). Allow self-pacing. Music at appropriate tempo (100-130 bpm for moderate exercise).
- Home exercise program safety: Clear floor of tripping hazards. Adequate lighting. Sturdy chair for support (no wheels). Non-slip flooring or yoga mat. Water within reach. Phone nearby for emergencies. First time: offer supervised instruction.
- Warm-up for older adults (10 min): March in place (2 min), Ankle mobility (circles, alphabet), Knee lifts (marching with high knees), Side steps with toe taps, Leg swings forward/back and side, Arm circles (small to large), Trunk rotations (slow, controlled), Neck rotations, Cat-cow (if floor/wall), Deep breathing (5 breaths).
- Cool-down (5-10 min): Slow walking or marching, Deep breathing with arm raises, Upper traps stretch (ear to shoulder), Pec stretch (doorway), Seated forward fold, Hamstring stretch (foot on chair), Quad stretch (standing, hold onto chair), Glute stretch (ankle to knee in chair).

SECTION 9: FALL PREVENTION
- Fall risk factors: Intrinsic: Muscle weakness (especially lower body), Gait and balance problems, Vision impairment, Cognitive impairment, Medications (especially sedatives, BP meds, psychotropics), Foot problems. Extrinsic: Environmental hazards (throw rugs, cords, clutter, poor lighting), Inappropriate footwear, Improper use of assistive devices.
- The Otago Exercise Program: Evidence-based fall prevention program developed in New Zealand. 17 exercises: 8 strength exercises (knee extensor, knee flexor, hip abductor, hip extensor, calf raises, toe raises, etc.) + 9 balance exercises (knee bends, backward walking, walking and turning, sideways walking, tandem stand, tandem walk, single leg stand, heel walking, toe walking, etc.). Performed 3x/week, 30 min. Reduces falls by 30-35% in community-dwelling older adults.
- Stepping On program: Multifaceted community-based fall prevention program. Focuses on strength and balance exercises, home hazard assessment, medication review, vision checks, safe footwear, and behavioral change strategies. 7-week group program with 1 follow-up home visit.
- Fear of falling: Affects 20-60% of older adults who have not fallen. It IS a risk factor for falls (reduces activity → deconditioning → weakness → falls). Interventions: Build confidence through successful exercise experiences. Education about fall risks. Balance training in safe environments.
- Home safety checklist: Remove throw rugs and secure edges of carpets. Install grab bars in shower and by toilet. Use non-slip mats in shower/tub. Ensure adequate lighting (night lights in hallway/bathroom). Keep pathways clear of cords and clutter. Use sturdy handrails on stairs. Store frequently used items at waist level (not high/low).
- Footwear for fall prevention: Low heel (<1 inch), firm sole (not too soft), slip-resistant outsole, good arch support, snug fit (not loose), preferably lace-up or velcro closure (not slip-on). Avoid: flip-flops, high heels, slippers without backs, walking socks without shoes.
- After a fall: Don't panic. Assess for injury before moving. Get up using log-roll technique (roll onto side, push up with arms, get on hands and knees, crawl to stable chair, use chair to stand). Seek medical attention if: hit head (even if no symptoms), severe pain, can't bear weight, taking blood thinners. Psychological recovery is important (don't let fear restrict activity).
- Vision and falls: Regular eye exams (yearly). Corrective lenses for distance and reading. Cataract surgery reduces falls by 40%. Avoid bifocal/progressive lenses when walking outdoors or on stairs (wear single-distance glasses for walking).
- Medications and falls: Review ALL medications annually (including OTC and supplements). Benzodiazepines increase fall risk 2-3x. Antihypertensives can cause orthostatic hypotension (dizziness when standing up). Blood glucose meds can cause hypoglycemic falls. Diuretics may cause urgency-related falls (rushing to bathroom).

SECTION 10: RESISTANCE TRAINING PROGRAM DESIGN - GENERAL TO ADVANCED
- Periodization models: Linear (traditional: increase load, decrease reps over 4-8 weeks), Block (focus on one quality for 2-4 weeks, then switch), Undulating/DUP (vary reps and loads within the same week), Conjugate (Westside: max effort day + dynamic effort day for same lifts).
- Autoregulation: Adjusting training load based on daily readiness. Methods: RPE-based (select load based on how heavy it feels), velocity-based (barbell speed sensors), AMRAP set (use last set to determine next session's load), repetitions in reserve (RIR). More responsive than fixed percentages.
- Complex training: Alternating a heavy strength exercise (3-5 reps at 85%+) with a plyometric exercise of similar movement pattern. Example: Barbell back squat → vertical jump. Box squat → broad jump. Exploits PAP (post-activation potentiation) — the strength set enhances the plyometric response.
- Cluster sets: Sets broken into mini-clusters with brief intra-set rest (15-30 seconds within a set). Example: 6 reps spread as 2+2+2 with 20 seconds rest between clusters. Allows more total reps at high loads (95%+ of 1RM) with better technique and bar speed. Used for power and maximal strength development.
- Blood flow restriction (BFR) training: Inflatable cuffs partially restrict arterial inflow and fully restrict venous outflow. Allows muscle growth with very light loads (20-30% 1RM). Useful for: rehab settings (joint protection), deload periods, older adults (low joint stress). Protocol: 75 reps total (30-15-15-15), 30 sec inter-set rest.
- Eccentric training: Emphasizing the lowering phase (30-50% faster eccentric than concentric). Greater muscle damage and growth stimulus. Supramaximal eccentrics (loads 105-130% of concentric 1RM) for tendon rehabilitation. Used in: Nordic hamstring curl, slow eccentrics, weight releasers.
- Isometric training: Muscular contraction without joint movement. Length-specific strength gains (strongest at the trained joint angle). Can produce 5-10% strength increase per 4-week block. Effective for overcoming sticking points. Protocol: 5-10 second maximal efforts, 3-5 reps, 3-5 angles.
- Velocity-based training (VBT): Using barbell speed to determine load, effort, and fatigue. Uses a linear position transducer or accelerometer. Zones: 1.3+ m/s (power), 0.8-1.2 m/s (strength-speed), 0.5-0.8 m/s (speed-strength), <0.5 m/s (maximal strength). A >20% drop in velocity across a set signals termination (enough volume).
- Rest interval science: Muscular endurance (15+ reps): 30-60 sec rest. Hypertrophy (8-12 reps): 60-90 sec rest. Strength (3-6 reps): 2-5 min rest. Power (1-3 reps): 3-5 min rest. Insufficient rest = more systemic fatigue, less quality work. Longer rest = more total volume at high loads.
- RPE based approach for endurance sports: Rate of Perceived Exertion on 1-10 scale. 1 = nothing, 5 = hard, 10 = maximum. Session RPE method (Foster et al.): multiply session duration × RPE for training load score (e.g., 60 min × 7 RPE = 420 AU). Track weekly loads. Avoid >10% spike week-over-week. Monitor acute:chronic workload ratio.

SECTION 11: PLYOMETRICS & AGILITY
- Plyometric progression (vertical): Level 1 (low intensity): ankle jumps, pogo jumps. Level 2: squat jumps, box jumps (low box). Level 3: depth jumps from 6-12 inch box. Level 4: depth jumps from 18+ inch box, weighted vest jumps. Level 5: single-leg jumps, barrier jumps.
- Plyometric progression (horizontal): Level 1: standing long jump, broad jumps. Level 2: hurdle jumps (consecutive broad jumps over low hurdles). Level 3: bounding (alternating leg, exaggerated running stride). Level 4: single-leg broad jumps. Level 5: multi-directional bounds (lateral, rotational).
- Landing mechanics: Key to injury prevention. Soft landing: hips and knees bend, weight over midfoot, feet shoulder-width apart, knees track over toes, torso upright. Avoid: stiff-legged landing, knees collapsing inward, landing flat-footed, excessive forward lean.
- Depth jump science: Stepping off a box and immediately jumping upon landing. Uses the stretch-shortening cycle. Optimal box height: 16-30 inches (lower for beginners, higher for advanced). The goal is to minimize ground contact time (ideally <0.25 seconds). Peak force during depth jumps can reach 3-5x bodyweight.
- Ground contact time: <0.1 sec = very fast SSC (stiffness dominant, like sprinting). 0.1-0.25 sec = fast SSC (plyometrics). >0.25 sec = slow SSC (strength dominant). Training the SSC involves both low (<0.1s) and moderate (0.15-0.25s) contact time work.
- Agility: Change of direction speed combined with reaction to a stimulus. Differs from pre-planned COD (pre-planned change of direction). True agility requires perception and decision-making (reacting to a defender, ball, or opponent). Drills: pro agility (5-10-5), T-drill, mirror drills, reactive cone drills.
- COD mechanics: Plant foot: last contact foot provides braking force. Angle of plant determines exit angle. Penultimate step (second-to-last step) lowers center of mass. Hip and knee flexion absorb braking forces. Inside leg pushes in new direction. Arm opposite direction helps reaccelerate.

SECTION 12: AQUATIC THERAPY & EXERCISE
- Buoyancy effects: Reduces body weight by 90% in chest-deep water and 50% in waist-deep water. Unloads joints (spine, hips, knees, ankles). Resistance increases with speed of movement. Water temperature: 83-86°F for therapy, 86-90°F for arthritis, 78-82°F for vigorous exercise.
- Hydrostatic pressure: Water pressure on the body reduces edema (swelling), improves venous return, reduces heart rate during exercise (compared to land at same intensity), and may improve kidney function. One of the unique benefits of water-based exercise.
- Deep water running: Using a flotation belt to run in deep water. Mimics land running form. Excellent cross-training for runners (maintains fitness while injured). Intensity: 85-90% of land running HR at same RPE (due to cooling effect of water and reduced gravitational demand).
- Ai Chi: Water-based adaptation of Tai Chi. Slow, continuous movements in waist-to-chest deep water. Combines breathing with progressive movements. Improves balance, flexibility, and relaxation. Particularly beneficial for older adults and those with chronic musculoskeletal conditions.
- Watsu: Water shiatsu (massage) performed in chest-deep warm water (95°F). Therapist supports the client and performs stretches and massage while floating. Used for pain management, stress reduction, and improved mobility. Contraindicated: open wounds, severe cardiovascular disease, uncontrolled epilepsy.
- Bad Ragaz method: Active-assisted aquatic exercise using buoyant cuffs and rings. Patient performs specific movements against resistance from water and therapist-guided patterns. Based on PNF principles (diagonal, rotational patterns). Used in neurological rehab (stroke, spinal cord injury, MS).
- Halliwick method: Swimming instruction and therapy for individuals with disabilities (originally developed for children with physical disabilities). 10-point program: mental adjustment, disengagement, vertical rotation, lateral rotation, combined rotation, upthrust, balance, gliding, turbulent gliding, simple progression, basic progression. Focus on rotational control and breath control in water.
- Water walking: Forward, backward, and sideways walking in chest-to-waist deep water. Variations: high knees, heel walking, toe walking, cross-over steps, grapevine. Add resistance: webbed gloves, water dumbbells, ankle weights (if no joint issues).

SECTION 13: SPINAL HEALTH & POSTURE
- Spine anatomy basics: 33 vertebrae (7 cervical, 12 thoracic, 5 lumbar, 5 sacral fused, 4 coccyx). Intervertebral discs: annulus fibrosus (outer ring) and nucleus pulposus (gelatinous center). Disc hydration: morning discs are 23% more hydrated and ~20% stiffer. Disc herniation: nucleus pushes through annulus.
- Posture myths debunked: There is no single "perfect" posture. Static posture is less important than postural variability (frequent position changes). "Text neck" is not proven to cause long-term damage. Thoracic "straightening" is not possible (kyphosis is natural). The health impact of posture is often overstated.
- Back pain epidemiology: 80% of adults experience back pain in their lifetime. Most acute back pain (90%) resolves in 6 weeks regardless of treatment. Recurrence rate: 40-80% within 1 year. Imaging (MRI/X-ray) for non-specific back pain is rarely indicated in first 6 weeks.
- Exercise for back pain: Research shows exercise is effective for both prevention and treatment. Core stabilization (transversus abdominis, multifidus). Motor control exercises. General exercise (walking, swimming) is as effective as specific exercises. Avoid: positions that reproduce sharp/radiating pain.
- Common back-safe modifications: Deadlift: trap bar > straight bar > sumo (reduces spinal shear). Squat: box squat > front squat > high bar > low bar (reduces lumbar load). Good mornings: avoid for disc issues. Standing overhead press: seated with back support reduces lumbar load.
- Core training basics: Transversus abdominis (deepest, corset-like), internal/external obliques (rotation), rectus abdominis (superficial, flexes trunk), quadratus lumborum (lateral stability), multifidus (segmental spinal stability), diaphragm (breathing + pressure). Exercises: dead bug (best for motor control), bird dog, side plank, front plank, pallof press.
- Intra-abdominal pressure: Pressurizing the abdominal cavity to stabilize the spine. Bracing technique: take a deep breath into the belly (360-degree expansion), hold, brace abdominal wall (like someone is about to punch you). Increases spinal stiffness. Use for heavy lifts (squats, deadlifts, carries). NOT the same as Valsalva (which is holding breath against a closed glottis).
- Breathing and core stability: Diaphragmatic breathing coordinates with the deep core. On exertion: the diaphragm and pelvic floor should move together (intra-abdominal pressure increases). With poor breathing mechanics: diaphragm is inhibited, pelvic floor doesn't engage, pressure leaks. Practice: 360-degree breathing (expand ribs + belly in all directions).
- Scoliosis and exercise: Scoliosis-specific exercises (Schroth method, SEAS approach) can reduce curve progression and improve posture. Key principles: elongation of the spine, rotational breathing into the concave side, muscular correction. These exercises are technique-specific and should be taught by a Schroth-certified physical therapist.
- Osteoporosis-safe modifications: Avoid spinal flexion (crunching forward motion). Avoid heavy axial loading of spine (squats with very heavy loads, heavy overhead press). Best exercises: back extension (prone raises), rows (strengthen postural muscles), hip hinge variations (glute bridges). Supplement with calcium (1200mg/day) and vitamin D (800-2000 IU/day) as needed.
- Sitting posture optimization: Support lumbar curve (small pillow or roll). Feet flat on floor. Hips slightly above knees (prevents posterior pelvic tilt). Screen at eye level. Elbows at 90 degrees. Vary positions: every 30 minutes, stand up or recline slightly. Standing desk breaks are helpful but don't stand all day either.
