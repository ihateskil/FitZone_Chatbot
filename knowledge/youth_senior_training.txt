===== YOUTH & OLDER ADULT TRAINING GUIDE =====

SECTION 1: YOUTH TRAINING BASICS (AGES 6-12)
- Children are NOT miniature adults. Their physiology, thermoregulation, and recovery differ significantly.
- Focus: Skill development, fun, movement variety, and lifelong fitness habits. NOT maximal strength or competition.
- Growth plate safety: Proper form and supervision are essential. Growth plate injuries from improper loading are rare with qualified supervision.
- Aerobic capacity: Children have higher relative aerobic capacity than adults but fatigue faster due to less efficient metabolism.
- Thermoregulation: Children sweat less, have higher surface-to-mass ratio, and are more prone to heat illness. Hydrate frequently, avoid extreme heat.
- Bone health: Weight-bearing exercise in youth is critical for peak bone mass development (achieved around age 30).
- Motor skill development: Fundamental movement skills (running, jumping, throwing, catching, balancing) should be developed before sport-specific skills.

SECTION 2: YOUTH STRENGTH TRAINING (AGES 12+)
- Resistance training is safe AND effective for youth when properly supervised and programmed.
- Benefits: Increased strength (30-50% improvement typical), improved body composition, enhanced sports performance, reduced injury risk.
- Age to start: When child can follow instructions and maintain attention (typically age 7-8 minimum). Bodyweight training first.
- Program design: 2-3x/week, full body, 8-12 exercises, 10-15 reps, focus on form over load.
- Injury rates: Youth resistance training injury rates are LOWER than many sports (soccer, basketball, football).
- Growth plate safety myth: Properly supervised resistance training does NOT stunt growth. This myth has been debunked by decades of research.
- The 4-stage model: Stage 1 (bodyweight mastery), Stage 2 (external load introduction), Stage 3 (basic strength program), Stage 4 (sport-specific).

SECTION 3: ADOLESCENT TRAINING (AGES 13-17)
- Puberty creates a window of trainability. Strength gains accelerate during Peak Height Velocity (PHV).
- PHV: Boys ~14 ± 1 year. Girls ~12 ± 1 year. This is when training adaptations are most dramatic.
- Growth spurts: During rapid growth, coordination can temporarily decrease (adolescent awkwardness). Focus on fundamentals.
- Osgood-Schlatter disease: Knee pain from traction at tibial tuberosity. Common in adolescent athletes. Modify load, avoid deep kneeling, strengthen quads and hamstrings.
- Scoliosis screening: Adolescent females should be screened. Asymmetrical loading may need modification.
- Periodization: 4-6 week blocks with progression. Deload weeks every 4-6 weeks.
- Overtraining risk: Adolescents can overtrain. Watch for: decreased performance, chronic fatigue, mood changes, loss of motivation.
- Sports specialization: Early specialization increases injury risk and burnout. Encourage multi-sport participation until age 15-16.

SECTION 4: OLDER ADULT TRAINING BASICS (AGES 65+)
- Aging causes: Sarcopenia (muscle loss of 3-8% per decade after 30), bone density loss, reduced flexibility, slower metabolism, balance decline.
- Benefits of training: Improved mobility and independence, fall prevention, cognitive function, cardiovascular health, metabolic health, mental health.
- Medical clearance: Required before starting a new exercise program. Screen for cardiovascular disease, joint replacements, medications affecting heart rate.
- Warm-up: 10-15 minutes minimum. Low-intensity movement, dynamic stretches, joint mobilizations.
- Cool-down: 10 minutes. Static stretches held 30-60 seconds. Helps maintain flexibility and reduce post-exercise soreness.

SECTION 5: STRENGTH TRAINING FOR OLDER ADULTS
- Strength training is the MOST important type of exercise for aging populations.
- Benefits: Increases muscle mass (combating sarcopenia), improves bone density, increases metabolic rate, improves glucose metabolism, enhances mobility.
- Frequency: 2-3x/week full body. Allow 48 hours recovery between sessions.
- Intensity: Moderate (RPE 5-7/10). 8-12 reps. Progress to heavier loads as tolerated.
- Key exercises: Squat pattern (chair squats), hip hinge (good mornings), push (wall push-ups), pull (band rows), loaded carries (farmer carries).
- Fall prevention: Strong legs and core reduce fall risk by 30-50%. Include standing work and balance exercises.
- Blood pressure: Avoid prolonged breath-holding (valsalva). Exhale on exertion. Monitor BP response.

SECTION 6: CARDIO FOR OLDER ADULTS
- Low-impact options: Walking, swimming, water aerobics, stationary cycling, elliptical, seated cardio.
- Frequency: 150 minutes moderate or 75 minutes vigorous cardio per week (WHO guidelines). Can be broken into 10-min bouts.
- Heart rate considerations: Some medications (beta-blockers) blunt heart rate response. Use RPE instead of HR zones.
- Bone health: Weight-bearing cardio (walking, stair climbing) is superior to non-weight bearing (cycling, swimming) for bone density.
- Fall risk: Treadmill walking has higher fall risk. Use handrails, walk on level ground when possible.

SECTION 7: MOBILITY & FLEXIBILITY FOR OLDER ADULTS
- Age-related changes: Decreased joint range of motion, stiffer tendons, reduced synovial fluid.
- Daily mobility: Gentle stretching each morning. Focus on hips, shoulders, spine, and ankles.
- Balance training: Stand on one foot (hold chair), heel-to-toe walk, tai chi, yoga.
- Posture: Age-related kyphosis (hunched back) can impair breathing and balance. Thoracic extension, chin tucks, wall angels.
- Arthritis: Movement improves joint health. Avoid high-impact during flares. Water exercise is excellent.

SECTION 8: CHRONIC CONDITIONS & EXERCISE
- Type 2 diabetes: Exercise improves insulin sensitivity for 24-72 hours post-exercise. Both cardio and strength training effective. Monitor blood glucose before/during/after.
- Osteoarthritis: Maintain joint mobility. Low-impact cardio, isometric strengthening. Avoid painful ranges. Heat before exercise, ice after.
- Cardiovascular disease: Cardiac rehabilitation protocols. Monitor intensity. Avoid heavy straining (valsalva). Gradual progression.
- Osteoporosis: Avoid spinal flexion (bending forward at waist) under load. Prefer axial loading (squats, carries) and low-impact weight-bearing.
- Hypertension: Moderate intensity exercise lowers BP 5-7 mmHg. Avoid heavy lifting (above RPE 7/10) until BP controlled.
- Dementia/Alzheimer's: Exercise is ONE of the few interventions shown to slow cognitive decline. Walking programs most studied.

SECTION 9: PROGRAM DESIGN FOR OLDER ADULTS
- FITT principles: Frequency 3-5x/week. Intensity moderate (able to talk during exercise). Time 30-60 min. Type mixed (strength, cardio, balance, flexibility).
- Session structure: Warm-up (10 min), Cardio (15-20 min), Strength (15-20 min), Balance/Flexibility (10 min), Cool-down (5 min).
- Progression: Increase duration before increasing intensity. Increase reps before increasing weight.
- Rate of progress: Older adults progress more slowly. Allow 2-4 weeks for initial adaptations. Celebrate small wins.
- Social component: Group exercise classes improve adherence. Community and social connection are important benefits.
- Safety: Have a chair nearby for support. Non-slip shoes, clear floor space. Adequate lighting. Stay hydrated.

SECTION 10: FALLS PREVENTION
- Falls are the leading cause of injury in adults 65+. 1 in 4 older adults fall each year.
- Key risk factors: Lower body weakness, vitamin D deficiency, balance difficulties, vision problems, medications (sedatives, BP meds), home hazards.
- Evidence-based programs: Otago Exercise Program (home-based strength and balance), Tai Chi: Moving for Better Balance, Stepping On.
- Exercises for fall prevention: Sit-to-stand (10 reps, build to 1-leg), tandem stance (heel-to-toe), single leg stance (aim for 30s), heel raises, toe raises, sideways walking.
- Home safety: Remove throw rugs, improve lighting, install grab bars in bathroom, use non-slip mats, keep pathways clear.
- Fear of falling: Can be as debilitating as falls themselves. Build confidence through progressive balance training.
- What to do after a fall: Don't panic. Check for injury. Get up safely (roll to side, use sturdy chair for support). Seek medical evaluation.
