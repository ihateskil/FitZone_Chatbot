===== MOBILITY, FLEXIBILITY & CORRECTIVE EXERCISE GUIDE =====

SECTION 1: MOBILITY VS FLEXIBILITY
- Flexibility: The ability of a muscle to lengthen passively (static range of motion).
- Mobility: The ability to move a joint actively through its full range of motion (ROM). Combines flexibility + strength + control.
- Mobility is more important than flexibility alone. A flexible but uncontrolled joint is at risk of injury.
- Test vs train: Assess mobility first, then train the specific deficits.

SECTION 2: STATIC STRETCHING
- Best for post-workout (cool-down). Holds of 30-60 seconds.
- Pre-workout static stretching CAN reduce maximal strength and power (by up to 5-10%) if held longer than 60 seconds.
- Dynamic stretching is preferred before workouts (leg swings, arm circles, torso twists).
- PNF stretching (proprioceptive neuromuscular facilitation): Contract muscle for 5-10s, relax, then stretch further. Most effective for increasing ROM.
- Frequency: Minimum 2-3x per week for each muscle group. 5-10 minutes per session.
- Never bounce during static stretches (ballistic stretching increases injury risk).

SECTION 3: DYNAMIC WARM-UP
- Purpose: Increase body temperature, activate nervous system, improve joint lubrication, practice movement patterns, reduce injury risk.
- Key components: 5-10 minutes of light cardio, followed by dynamic stretches and sport-specific movements.
- Lower body warm-up: Leg swings (forward and lateral), walking lunges, butt kicks, high knees, hip circles, bodyweight squats.
- Upper body warm-up: Arm circles, shoulder rotations, cat-cow, thoracic rotations, band pull-aparts.
- RAMP protocol: Raise (body temp, 3-5 min light cardio), Activate (key muscles, glute bridges, band work), Mobilize (joints, dynamic stretches, CARs), Potentiate (sport-specific drills, 1-2 working sets at 50-70% intensity).
- Total warm-up time: 10-15 minutes. Longer for older athletes, colder environments, and first session of the day.
- General vs specific warm-up: General (increase core temp, 5 min) then specific (movements that mimic the workout, 5-10 min). Example: before squats, do bodyweight squats, goblet squats, then empty bar squats.

SECTION 4: COMMON MOBILITY ISSUES
- Ankle mobility: Limited dorsiflexion causes squat issues (heels lifting, excessive forward lean, knees caving). Fix: Calf stretching, ankle CARs (controlled articular rotations), banded mobilizations (pull ankle forward with band), tibialis raises, deep squat holds.
- Hip mobility: Tight hip flexors from prolonged sitting. Fix: Hip flexor lunges (couch stretch), 90/90 hip rotations, frog stretch, hip CARs, pigeon pose, elephant walk.
- Thoracic spine mobility: Rounded upper back from desk work. Fix: Thoracic extensions over foam roller (3 positions: upper, mid, lower T-spine), open books, cat-cow, thread-the-needle, T-spine rotations in half-kneeling.
- Shoulder mobility: Tight pecs and lats limit overhead reaching. Fix: Doorway pec stretch (vary arm height for different fibers), lat stretch (holding onto rack), banded shoulder dislocates, wall slides, floor angels.
- Wrist mobility: Important for push-ups, handstands, and front rack position in squats. Fix: Wrist CARs, prayer stretch, finger extensions, wrist flexor/extensor stretches.
- Cervical spine (neck): Forward head posture from screen use. Fix: Chin tucks (2-3 sets of 10, held 3-5 sec), neck rotations, side-bending stretches, suboccipital release with lacrosse ball.
- Assessment: Overhead squat test reveals mobility deficits in real time. Watch for: arms falling forward (tight lats/pecs, weak upper back), torso leaning forward (poor ankle/hip mobility), knees caving (weak glutes, poor ankle mobility), lower back arching (tight hip flexors, weak core).

SECTION 5: FOAM ROLLING & SELF-MYOFASCIAL RELEASE
- Purpose: Reduce muscle tension, improve blood flow, break up adhesions.
- Technique: Roll slowly over the muscle. Pause on tender spots for 30-60 seconds. Breath deeply during pause.
- Contraindications: Do NOT roll directly over bony areas, joints, or injured tissue.
- Best areas to roll: Quads, hamstrings, glutes, upper back (not lower back), calves, IT band (lateral quad).
- Frequency: Before and after workouts. Pre-workout rolling improves ROM temporarily.
- Lacrosse ball: For targeted release in shoulders, glutes, feet, and upper back.

SECTION 6: CORRECTIVE EXERCISE
- NASM Corrective Exercise Continuum: Inhibit → Lengthen → Activate → Integrate.
- Step 1 (Inhibit): Foam roll or massage tight/overactive muscles.
- Step 2 (Lengthen): Stretch the same muscles.
- Step 3 (Activate): Strengthen weak/underactive muscles.
- Step 4 (Integrate): Perform compound movements that use both the inhibited and activated muscles.
- Upper crossed syndrome: Tight pecs and upper traps, weak deep neck flexors and lower traps. Fix: Pec stretch, chin tucks, face pulls, rows.
- Lower crossed syndrome: Tight hip flexors and lumbar extensors, weak glutes and abs. Fix: Hip flexor stretch, glute bridges, planks, dead bugs.

SECTION 7: KEY MOBILITY ROUTINES
- Morning mobility (5 min): Cat-cow, child's pose, thoracic rotations, hip circles, ankle circles, neck rotations, deep breathing.
- Pre-workout dynamic warm-up (10 min): Jumping jacks, leg swings, walking lunges with twist, torso rotations, arm circles, bodyweight squats with reach.
- Post-workout cool-down (5-10 min): Hold each stretch 30-45 seconds. Hamstring stretch, quad stretch, glute stretch, chest stretch, lat stretch, cat-cow.
- Desk worker fixes (hourly): Chin tucks, shoulder rolls, wrist stretches, standing hip flexor stretch, thoracic extension.

SECTION 8: BREATHING & DIAPHRAGM
- Diaphragmatic breathing: Breathe into the belly, not the chest. Inhale through nose for 4 counts, exhale through mouth for 6 counts.
- 360-degree breathing: Expand the ribcage in all directions (front, sides, back).
- Breath timing in lifting: Exhale on the concentric (hard) phase, inhale on the eccentric (easy) phase. Hold breath (valsalva) during heavy compound lifts under 5 reps.
- Nose breathing during cardio: Nasal breathing filters air, releases nitric oxide, improves oxygen uptake efficiency.
- The physiological sigh: Double inhale through nose followed by long exhale through mouth. Fastest way to calm the nervous system.

SECTION 9: JOINT-SPECIFIC CARE
- Knees: Most common injury site in fitness. Quad/hamstring balance important. Patellar tracking issues from weak VMO (vastus medialis oblique). Fix: Terminal knee extensions, step-ups.
- Lower back: Most common chronic pain area. Core stability and hip mobility are keys. McGill Big 3: Curl-up, side plank, bird dog.
- Shoulders: Most common upper body injury. Rotator cuff health: External rotations, face pulls, Y-T-W-L raises. Avoid pressing behind neck.
- Elbows: Golfer's elbow (medial) and tennis elbow (lateral). Often from grip issues. Fix: Eccentric wrist curls/extensions, reduce grip tension, improve technique.
- Hips: Labral tears and FAI (femoroacetabular impingement). Fix: Avoid deep squats if painful, strengthen glute medius, improve hip CARs.

SECTION 10: FLEXIBILITY STANDARDS
- Toe touch: Sit and reach test. Palms past toes = good. Fingertips to toes = average. Palms at shins = poor.
- Squat depth: Thighs parallel to ground minimum, below parallel ideal. Ankles, hips, and T-spine all contribute.
- Overhead squat test: Hold PVC pipe overhead, squat down. Pipe should stay over mid-foot, torso upright. Watch for: arms falling forward, heels lifting, knees caving, excessive forward lean.
- Shoulder flexion: Arms straight overhead against wall. 180 degrees = full ROM. 140-150 is common deficit in desk workers.
- Hip extension: Thomas test. Lying on back, pull one knee to chest. Other leg should remain flat on table. If it lifts, hip flexors are tight.
- Hip internal rotation: Sitting, knees at 90 degrees. Rotate lower leg outward. 45 degrees each side is normal. Less than 30 degrees indicates restriction.
- Ankle dorsiflexion: Knee-to-wall test. With foot flat, knee should touch wall 4-6 inches (10-15 cm) from toes while heel stays down. Less than 4 inches indicates ankle mobility deficit.

SECTION 11: MOBILITY PROGRAMMING
- Frequency: Daily for 5-15 minutes is better than 60 minutes once per week. Consistency over intensity.
- When to mobilize: Morning (stiff, focus on movement quality and circulation), Pre-workout (dynamic, sport-specific), Post-workout (static holds, PNF, greatest ROM gains from warm tissue), Evening (de-stress, gentle, deep breathing).
- Mobility progression: Start with CARs (controlled articular rotations) for awareness, then targeted drills for specific deficits, then integrate into warm-ups and cool-downs.
- Maintenance vs corrective: Maintenance mobility (10-15 min daily, full body, low intensity). Corrective mobility (20-30 min daily, targeted to specific deficits, higher intensity). Allocate 80% to maintenance, 20% to corrective when no major deficits exist.
- Mobility tools: Lacrosse ball (targeted release), foam roller (general release), resistance bands (mobilization flossing), yoga blocks (support for stretches), PVC pipe (overhead position training).

SECTION 12: FLEXIBILITY TRAINING METHODS
- Static stretching: Hold at end-range without pain. 30-60 second holds, 2-4 sets per muscle group. Best performed on warm muscles (post-workout or after warm-up). Total weekly dose: 5-10 minutes per muscle group.
- Dynamic stretching: Controlled movement through ROM. 10-15 reps per movement. Best pre-workout. Examples: leg swings, torso twists, cat-cow, walking lunges with rotation.
- PNF stretching: Contract-relax (CR): contract target muscle for 5-7 seconds, relax, then stretch further. Contract-relax-antagonist-contract (CRAC): same as CR then contract opposing muscle. Most effective method for acute ROM increase. Best with partner or using band/towel.
- Loaded stretching: Hold end-range position with light weight (5-10% body weight) for 60-120 seconds. Examples: weighted pigeon pose, weighted splits, Jefferson curls. May produce longer-lasting ROM gains than unloaded stretching.
- Eccentric stretching (loaded): Slow, controlled lengthening of muscle under tension. Example: Romanian deadlift for hamstring length. Combines strength and flexibility training.
- Combining methods: Dynamic warm-up → main training → static or PNF stretching post-workout. 3-5 days per week for consistent progress.

SECTION 13: MOBILITY FOR SPECIFIC ACTIVITIES
- Squat mobility: Ankle dorsiflexion (banded distraction, calf stretch, deep squat holds), Hip internal rotation (90/90, couch stretch), T-spine extension (foam roller, open books), Bar position comfort (shoulder and lat mobility).
- Deadlift mobility: Hamstring length (RDLs, Jefferson curls, seated hamstring stretch), Hip hinge patterning (wall drill, dowel hip hinge), Ankle ROM (not critical for conventional unless sumo stance).
- Overhead press mobility: Shoulder flexion (wall slides, floor angels, banded dislocates), T-spine extension, Lat length (overhead banded lat stretch, kneeling lat stretch).
- Running mobility: Hip flexor length (couch stretch), Hip extension (glute bridge, single-leg bridge), Ankle ROM (calf stretch, tibialis raise), core stability for pelvic control.
- Swimming mobility: Shoulder internal/external rotation (banded rotations), T-spine rotation, Lat length, Ankle plantarflexion (pointed toes for kicking).
