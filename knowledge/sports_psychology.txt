===== SPORTS PSYCHOLOGY & MINDSET GUIDE =====

SECTION 1: GOAL SETTING
- SMART goals: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound.
- Process goals vs outcome goals: Focus on process goals (e.g., "train 4x/week") not just outcome goals (e.g., "lose 10kg"). Process goals are controllable.
- Short-term (daily/weekly), medium-term (monthly), long-term (yearly). Each short-term goal should ladder up to the next tier.
- Implementation intentions: "If [situation], then I will [behavior]." Increases goal adherence by 2-3x.
- Goal autonomy: Intrinsic motivation (doing it for yourself) beats extrinsic motivation (doing it for others/validation).

SECTION 2: MOTIVATION
- Intrinsic vs extrinsic motivation: Intrinsic (enjoyment, challenge, curiosity) is more sustainable. Extrinsic (rewards, praise, avoidance of shame) works short-term.
- Motivation curve: Motivation is highest at the start and fluctuates. Discipline bridges the gap when motivation is low.
- The 2-minute rule: Make the first step so easy it's impossible to say no. "Put on workout clothes" not "run 5K."
- Dopamine and habit loops: Cue → craving → response → reward. Stack rewards by pairing exercise with something enjoyable.
- Identity-based habits: Instead of "I want to lose weight," think "I am the type of person who values health and trains consistently."

SECTION 3: MENTAL TOUGHNESS
- Definition: The ability to persist through discomfort, adversity, and pressure.
- Components of mental toughness: Commitment (sticking to goals), Control (regulating emotions), Challenge (seeing difficulty as opportunity), Confidence (belief in abilities).
- Training mental toughness: Progressive exposure to discomfort. Cold showers, finishing reps when fatigued, maintaining form when tired.
- Self-talk: Replace negative ("I can't do this") with instructional ("Keep your chest up") or motivational ("One more rep"). Third-person self-talk is most effective.
- Visualization: Mentally rehearse the workout or competition. Imagine the sensations, movements, and success outcome. 5-10 minutes before training.

SECTION 4: FOCUS & CONCENTRATION
- Internal focus: Attention on bodily sensations (muscle tension, breathing). Best for technique work.
- External focus: Attention on the environment or outcome (the bar, the finish line). Generally produces better performance.
- Associative focus: Focusing on body signals during exercise. Useful for pacing and form.
- Dissociative focus: Distracting from body signals (music, podcast, counting). Useful for low-to-moderate intensity work.
- Flow state: Complete absorption in the activity. Time distortion. Occurs when challenge matches skill level.

SECTION 5: ANXIETY & PERFORMANCE
- Yerkes-Dodson Law: Moderate anxiety improves performance. Too little or too much impairs it.
- Pre-competition routine: A consistent 15-30 minute warm-up routine reduces anxiety and improves readiness.
- Breathing techniques: Box breathing (4-4-4-4), tactical breathing (4-4-4), diaphragmatic breathing.
- Catastrophizing: Challenge irrational fears. "What's the worst that could happen?" vs "What's most likely to happen?"
- Acceptance: Acknowledge nervousness without judgment. "I feel anxious and that's normal. I can perform well even while anxious."

SECTION 6: HABIT FORMATION
- The habit loop: Cue (trigger) → Routine (behavior) → Reward (benefit).
- Atomic Habits framework (James Clear): Make it obvious (cue), attractive (craving), easy (response), satisfying (reward). Invert for breaking bad habits.
- Habit stacking: "After [current habit], I will [new habit]." E.g., "After I brush my teeth, I will lay out my gym clothes."
- Streak tracking: Maintaining a visual record of consistency. Never miss twice in a row.
- 21/66/90 rule: Takes ~66 days on average for a behavior to become automatic (range 18-254 days depending on complexity).

SECTION 7: DEALING WITH SETBACKS
- The setback is not failure: Missing one workout doesn't ruin progress. The response determines long-term success.
- Forgiveness: Self-blame after a missed workout reduces motivation. Self-compassion increases it.
- Bounce-back protocol: Identify the obstacle, adjust the plan, schedule immediately, execute.
- Injury psychology: Focus on what you CAN do (modified training, rehab exercises) not what you can't.
- Plateau management: Periodize training, vary stimulus, track different metrics, trust the process.

SECTION 8: SOCIAL SUPPORT & ACCOUNTABILITY
- Training partners: Increase adherence by 30-50%. Provide motivation, spotting, and friendly competition.
- Coaches and personal trainers: Provide expertise, accountability, program design, form feedback.
- Online communities: Can provide support but watch for comparison trap and misinformation.
- Public commitment: Announcing goals increases accountability but can lead to premature satisfaction (social reality vs achieved reality).

SECTION 9: BODY IMAGE & SELF-PERCEPTION
- Body dysmorphia: Distorted perception of one's appearance. Common in bodybuilding and fitness cultures.
- Performance-based vs appearance-based goals: Performance goals build long-term satisfaction. Appearance-only goals often lead to dissatisfaction.
- Comparison trap: Compare yourself to your past self, not others. Everyone's genetics, lifestyle, and starting point are different.
- Progress photos and measurements: Objective data helps counteract subjective self-perception. Take monthly, not daily.
- Non-scale victories: Better sleep, more energy, improved mood, stronger lifts, better endurance, clothes fitting differently.

SECTION 10: SLEEP & RECOVERY PSYCHOLOGY
- Sleep is when the brain processes learning and adaptation. Poor sleep impairs motor skill acquisition.
- Revenge bedtime procrastination: Staying up late to reclaim personal time. Undermines training recovery.
- Rest days: Active recovery improves adherence long-term. Schedule rest like you schedule training.
- Mental fatigue: Cognitive load from work/life impairs physical performance. Warm-up protocols should include mental activation.
- Periodization of life stressors: During high-stress periods, maintain training but reduce expectations and volume.
