===== WOMEN'S FITNESS & PHYSIOLOGY GUIDE =====

SECTION 1: MENSTRUAL CYCLE & TRAINING
- Average cycle: 28 days (range 21-45 for some women). Phases: Follicular (days 1-14) and Luteal (days 15-28).
- Follicular phase (days 1-14): Estrogen rising, progesterone low. Better recovery, lower RPE at same workload, higher strength output. Good for heavy strength training and PR attempts.
- Menses (days 1-5): Estrogen and progesterone both low. Some women feel fatigued, others feel fine. No automatic need to reduce training unless symptoms require it.
- Ovulation (~day 14): Peak estrogen. Highest potential strength and power output. Increased injury risk due to higher ligament laxity (relaxin hormone peaks). Warm up thoroughly.
- Luteal phase (days 15-28): Progesterone dominant. Higher resting HR (+3-7 bpm), body temperature (+0.3-0.5 C), perceived exertion. Recovery may be slower. Fat oxidation increases.
- Late luteal / PMS (days 25-28): Potential for decreased performance, increased fatigue, water retention, mood changes. Reduce volume and intensity expectations. This is not weakness; it's physiology.
- Cycle syncing training: Not strictly necessary but can optimize comfort and results. Adjust intensity based on how you feel each phase. Tracking your cycle helps plan training peaks around follicular phase.
- Birth control: Oral contraceptives (combined pill) suppress the natural cycle. Progestin-only methods may cause irregular bleeding. Performance effects vary by pill type and individual. Some women experience strength decrease on hormonal contraceptives.
- Tracking: Use apps or journal to track cycle phases, energy, performance, and symptoms. 3-6 months of data reveals personal patterns.
- Myth busting: Women CAN train hard during their period. Some of the best performances in sports history occurred during menstruation.

SECTION 2: HORMONAL CONSIDERATIONS
- Women have 10-20x less testosterone than men, but are more sensitive to its effects.
- Estrogen: Promotes fat storage in hips/thighs, aids muscle recovery, protective for joints and bone density.
- Progesterone: Can increase body temperature, heart rate, and perceived effort during exercise.
- Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S): Low energy availability leads to menstrual dysfunction, bone density loss, weakened immune function. Serious health risk.
- The Female Athlete Triad: Low energy availability (with or without eating disorder) → Menstrual dysfunction → Low bone mineral density.

SECTION 3: STRENGTH TRAINING FOR WOMEN
- Women respond to strength training similarly to men (relative to muscle mass gains). Same rep ranges, programming principles apply.
- Absolute strength: Women average ~50-70% of male strength upper body, ~60-80% lower body.
- Relative strength (per kg bodyweight): The gap narrows to ~15-30% difference.
- Lower body dominance: Women typically have stronger lower bodies relative to upper body compared to men.
- Training frequency: 2-4x/week full body or split routines. Same principles of progressive overload apply.
- Muscle growth: Women can build significant muscle (hypertrophy). "Bulky" myth: women lack the hormonal profile to get bulky without intentional effort.

SECTION 4: CARDIO & ENDURANCE
- Women have ~5-10% lower VO2 max than men (scaled to body size). When scaled to fat-free mass, the gap narrows.
- Women have higher body fat percentage essential for hormonal function. 20-32% is healthy range for women (vs 10-22% for men).
- Fat oxidation: Women oxidize more fat and less carbohydrate during exercise compared to men at the same relative intensity.
- Heat regulation: Women have more efficient cooling (more sweat glands per area, larger surface-to-mass ratio).
- Anemia risk: Women have higher iron needs due to menstruation. Iron deficiency impairs endurance performance.

SECTION 5: BODY COMPOSITION
- Healthy body fat for women: Athletic (17-24%), Fitness (25-31%), Acceptable (32-37%). Below 12-15% can disrupt menstrual function.
- Fat storage: Women store more subcutaneous fat (especially hips and thighs) vs men store more visceral fat (belly).
- Weight loss: Women generally lose weight slower than men due to lower BMR and different hormonal response to calorie restriction.
- Spot reduction is a myth. Fat loss occurs systematically, not from specific exercises or areas.
- Body recomposition: Building muscle while losing fat is possible for beginners and those returning from a break.

SECTION 6: PREGNANCY & POSTPARTUM
- First trimester: Continue pre-pregnancy exercise if comfortable. Avoid overheating and high impact.
- Second trimester: Modified planks (avoid lying on back after 20 weeks). Avoid contact sports, fall risk activities.
- Third trimester: Lower impact exercise. Walking, swimming, prenatal yoga, stationary cycling.
- Diastasis recti: Separation of abdominal muscles common in pregnancy. Avoid crunches and twists. Modify with TVA activation and core bracing.
- Postpartum (0-6 weeks): Pelvic floor recovery. Gentle walking, deep breathing, pelvic tilts.
- Postpartum (6-12 weeks): Return to exercise gradually. Avoid heavy lifting until pelvic floor is cleared by physio.
- Breastfeeding: Additional 300-500 kcal/day needs. Stay hydrated. No performance impact from moderate exercise.

SECTION 7: PELVIC FLOOR HEALTH
- Functions: Supports bladder, bowel, and uterus. Plays role in continence, sexual function, and core stability.
- Weak pelvic floor signs: Urinary incontinence during exercise (leaking while running, jumping, lifting), heaviness, reduced sensation.
- Keels exercises: Contract pelvic floor (as if stopping urine flow) for 3-10 seconds. Relax fully between contractions. 10 reps, 3x/day.
- The Knack: Brace pelvic floor before sudden exertion (coughing, sneezing, heavy lifting).
- Avoid: Holding breath during exertion (increases intra-abdominal pressure on pelvic floor).

SECTION 8: BONE HEALTH
- Peak bone density achieved around age 30. Women lose bone density faster after menopause.
- Weight-bearing exercise is the best stimulus for bone density. Running, jumping, and lifting are effective.
- Impact forces of 2-4x body weight are ideal for bone stimulation. Walking alone may not be sufficient.
- High-risk sports for bone density: Swimming and cycling (non-weight bearing) do not improve bone density.
- Osteoporosis prevention: High-impact activity before menopause, strength training at all ages. Adequate calcium (1000-1200mg/day) and vitamin D (600-800 IU/day).

SECTION 9: MENOPAUSE & TRAINING
- Menopause (average age 51): Estrogen drops significantly. Fat redistribution from hips/thighs to abdomen.
- Training considerations: Strength training becomes MORE important (maintains muscle mass, bone density, metabolic rate).
- Hot flashes: Can be triggered by exercise. Dress in layers, train in cool environments, hydrate well.
- Joint pain: Estrogen decline affects joint lubrication and collagen synthesis. Warm up longer, consider joint supps (collagen, omega-3).
- Sleep disruption: Night-time hot flashes affect recovery. Prioritize sleep hygiene.
- Protein needs: May increase to 1.4-2.0g/kg to counteract anabolic resistance.

SECTION 10: SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS
- Eating disorders: Higher prevalence in women's fitness. Watch for orthorexia (obsession with clean eating), overtraining, body dysmorphia.
- PCOS (Polycystic Ovary Syndrome): ~10% of women. Insulin resistance common. Strength training and HIIT particularly beneficial. 
- Endometriosis: May affect training around menstruation. Modify intensity based on pain. Focus on gentle movement during flares.
- Iron deficiency: More common in female athletes. Symptoms: fatigue, poor recovery, shortness of breath. Test ferritin levels (target >30 ng/mL for athletes). Supplementation may be needed.
- ACL injury risk: Women have 2-8x higher ACL tear rate than men in sports. Prevention: hamstring strengthening (Nordic curls), landing mechanics (soft landings, knee over toe alignment), plyometric training (progressions from low to high intensity), core stability, neuromuscular training programs (FIFA 11+, PEP program).

SECTION 11: NUTRITION CONSIDERATIONS FOR WOMEN
- Iron needs: 18mg/day (pre-menopausal). Heme iron (meat) absorbs 2-3x better than non-heme (plants). Pair plant iron with vitamin C (citrus, peppers) to boost absorption.
- Calcium needs: 1000mg/day (1200mg for teens and post-menopausal). Critical for bone density. Dairy, fortified plant milks, leafy greens, canned fish with bones.
- Folate (B9): 400-800 mcg/day for women of childbearing age. Critical for fetal development. Sources: leafy greens, legumes, fortified grains.
- Protein needs: Same as men relative to body weight (1.6-2.2g/kg for muscle building). Women often undereat protein relative to needs.
- Carbohydrate needs: Women may benefit from slightly higher carb intake during luteal phase (increased glycogen utilization, higher resting metabolic rate). Add 100-200 kcal of carbs in the week before menstruation.
- Fat needs: Minimum 0.5-1g/kg for hormonal health. Very low fat diets (<15% calories from fat) can disrupt menstrual function.
- Hydration: Women have lower total body water than men. Thirst mechanisms differ. Drink to thirst, increase intake during luteal phase.
- Caffeine: May affect PMS symptoms and breast tenderness in some women. 200-300mg moderate consumption is safe for most.

SECTION 12: BODY IMAGE & PSYCHOLOGICAL FACTORS
- Social media influence: Comparison to filtered/edited images is a major source of body dissatisfaction. Coach clients to curate feeds toward educational content, not inspiration images.
- The "bulky" myth: Women lack the hormonal profile (10-20x less testosterone) to accidentally get bulky. Visible muscle requires years of intentional training and surplus calories.
- Performance focus over appearance: Clients who focus on performance markers (strength, endurance, skill) have better body satisfaction and training adherence than those focused on appearance.
- Scale weight fluctuations: Women's weight fluctuates 2-5 lbs during a menstrual cycle due to water retention, glycogen storage, and hormonal changes. Track trends (monthly averages), not daily numbers.
- Eating disorder red flags: Extreme calorie restriction, compulsive exercise, ritualistic eating, avoidance of social eating, intense fear of weight gain, distorted body perception. Refer to specialist.
- Coaching approach: Affirm effort and consistency. Never comment on body size or shape unprompted. Use neutral language: "You're getting stronger" not "You're looking better."
- Body image and athletics: Female athletes in aesthetic sports (gymnastics, figure skating, dance, bodybuilding) face higher body image pressure and disordered eating rates.

SECTION 13: LIFESPAN TRAINING FOR WOMEN
- Teens (13-19): Focus on technique, movement skill development, and building exercise habit. Emphasize bone density building (jumping, impact). Avoid excessive specialization.
- Early adulthood (20-35): Peak bone density window (ends ~age 30). Peak muscle building potential. Establish training consistency. Manage life transitions (career, relationships, pregnancy).
- Pregnancy (any age): Continue training as able. Modify intensity in third trimester. Pelvic floor training essential. Diastasis recti prevention (avoid crunches, control core loading).
- Perimenopause (35-50): Hormonal fluctuations begin 5-10 years before menopause. Strength training becomes critical. Protein intake should increase to 2g/kg. Sleep and stress management priorities.
- Menopause (50-60): Estrogen drops dramatically. Fat redistribution to abdomen. Accelerated bone density loss (1-2% per year in first 5 years post-menopause). Strength training 3-4x/week non-negotiable. Impact training for bones. Protein 2-2.4g/kg.
- Older adult (60+): Fall prevention is primary goal. Balance training (single leg stance, tai chi), lower body strength for mobility, bone density maintenance. Modify intensity for joint health.
